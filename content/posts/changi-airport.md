---
title: "On the peculiar optimism of Changi Airport"
date: 2026-02-28
tags: ["singapore", "observations"]
---

There is a waterfall in Terminal 1. Not a decorative trickle behind glass — a proper waterfall, four storeys tall, ringed by orchids and ferns, in the middle of an airport. The first time I came home after years abroad, I stood in front of it for longer than was probably normal.

Singapore does this thing where it treats civic infrastructure as a form of argument. The waterfall is not merely aesthetic. It is a proposition: that a place can be efficient and also beautiful, functional and also humane. That you do not have to choose.

I have been thinking about this lately, watching other cities manage their airports — the grey carpets, the malfunctioning travelators, the sandwiches wrapped in cellophane under fluorescent light. There is a theory that you can read the ambitions of a government in its public spaces. If so, what do those airports say?

The Jewel opened in 2019, just before everything changed. A glass dome, an indoor forest, a waterfall called the Rain Vortex that is the tallest indoor waterfall in the world. Absurd, really. And yet.

I keep coming back to the question of what it means for a small country to insist on this kind of excess — not in the sense of waste, but in the sense of surplus care. To build more than is strictly necessary. To make the place you land feel like somewhere worth arriving.
